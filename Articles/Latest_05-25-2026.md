## 1. Pope Leo XIV Releases AI Encyclical "Magnifica Humanitas" Alongside Anthropic Co-Founder

**Source:** [Vatican News](https://www.vaticannews.va/en/pope/news/2026-05/pope-leo-xiv-first-encyclical-magnifica-humanitas.html) | [PBS NewsHour](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/pope-leo-xiv-to-launch-his-first-encylical-a-document-on-artificial-intelligence-with-anthropics-co-founder) | [America Magazine](https://www.americamagazine.org/vatican-dispatch/2026/05/18/pope-leo-encyclical-artifical-intelligence-anthropic/)

Pope Leo XIV published his first encyclical, *Magnifica Humanitas* ("Magnificent Humanity"), on May 25, 2026 — the 135th anniversary of Pope Leo XIII's landmark *Rerum Novarum*. The document is centered on "the protection of the human person in the age of artificial intelligence." In a striking pairing of faith and technology, Anthropic co-founder and interpretability research head Christopher Olah joined the Pope at the presentation alongside Vatican theologians. Leo XIV frames AI as the defining challenge of our era — much like industrialization was in the 19th century — and positions the Church as a moral stakeholder in its development.

## 2. OpenAI Reasoning Model Autonomously Solves 80-Year-Old Erdős Geometry Problem

**Source:** [OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/model-disproves-discrete-geometry-conjecture/) | [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/20/openai-claims-it-solved-an-80-year-old-math-problem-for-real-this-time/) | [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/ai-just-solved-an-80-year-old-erdos-problem-and-mathematicians-are-amazed/)

OpenAI announced that a general-purpose reasoning model independently disproved the planar unit distance conjecture — an open problem posed by mathematician Paul Erdős in 1946. The problem asks how many unit-distance pairs can exist among *n* points in a plane; for decades, square grids were assumed optimal. The model discovered novel constructions using deep algebraic number theory that surpass square grids, marking the first time an AI has autonomously resolved a prominent open mathematical problem. The proof has been reviewed by top mathematicians including Timothy Gowers and Noga Alon, though full peer review is pending.

## 3. Google I/O Drops Gemini 3.5 Flash, Spark Agent, and XR Glasses

**Source:** [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/19/google-ai-ultra-gemini-spark-omni.html) | [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/21/google-ai-anthropic-openai-war)

At Google I/O, the company unveiled a sweeping AI product refresh. Gemini 3.5 Flash delivers frontier-class performance at roughly one-third the price of comparable models. Gemini Spark, a new general-purpose agent, can reason across connected apps and is rolling out to Google AI Ultra subscribers. Google is also redesigning its core search to handle long-form conversational queries alongside traditional searches, and YouTube is adding an "Ask YouTube" feature with blended text and video results. Google also showcased prototype XR glasses powered by its AI stack, signaling a renewed push into spatial computing.

## 4. Anthropic On Track for First Profitable Quarter; Inks $1.25B/Month SpaceX Compute Deal

**Source:** [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/21/ai-news-cycle-openai-anthropic-spacex)

Anthropic's explosive revenue growth has the company on track for its first profitable quarter, with Q2 revenue projected to more than double to $10.9 billion and an estimated $559 million operating profit — arriving two years ahead of internal projections. ## 5. Trump Administration Moves on AI Oversight: CEOs Summoned for Executive Order Signing

**Source:** [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/05/ai-oversight-trump-google-microsoft-xai.html)

The Trump administration summoned top tech CEOs to Washington for the signing of an AI executive order that is expected to establish a voluntary framework requiring labs to share new models with the government 90 days before public release. The order also directs testing of models from Google, Microsoft, and xAI. The framework is voluntary in nature — an earlier, more stringent version that would have created mandatory pre-release vetting was canceled after the administration determined it risked undermining America's competitive edge against China in AI development.
