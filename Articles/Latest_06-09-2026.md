## 3. Microsoft Project Polaris Replaces GPT-4 in GitHub Copilot

**Source:** [TechTimes](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317596/20260602/github-copilot-replaces-gpt-4-project-polaris-ships-multi-agent-vs-code-build.htm) | [AI Tool Briefing](https://aitoolbriefing.com/industry/microsoft-build-2026-copilot-project-polaris/)

Announced at Microsoft Build 2026 on June 2, **Project Polaris** is Microsoft's homegrown AI coding model built to replace GPT-4 Turbo as the default engine for all GitHub Copilot subscribers starting August 2026. The model uses a mixture-of-experts architecture with specialized sub-modules for different programming languages and frameworks, runs on Microsoft's custom Maia AI accelerators inside Azure, and reportedly outperforms GPT-4 Turbo on standard coding benchmarks. Pro subscribers get multi-file context up to 100,000 lines and autonomous test generation. The shift ends Microsoft's dependence on OpenAI models following the dissolution of their seven-year exclusive partnership in April.

## 4. Congress Drops 269-Page AI Bill That Could Override State Laws

**Source:** [Roll Call](https://rollcall.com/2026/06/04/bipartisan-ai-draft-proposes-three-year-preemption-of-state-laws/) | [TechTimes](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/317903/20260606/federal-ai-regulation-bill-freezes-state-consumer-protections-three-years-sparks-revolt.htm) | [ACLU](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/aclu-reacts-to-draft-bipartisan-ai-bill-that-would-preempt-state-laws)

Reps. Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released the **Great American Artificial Intelligence Act of 2026**, a bipartisan 269-page discussion draft that would bar states from enacting new laws governing how AI systems are *built* for at least three years. Large frontier AI developers with over $500 million in gross annual revenue would face mandatory semi-annual third-party audits and must publish risk frameworks disclosing whether their models could pose "catastrophic risk" (defined as posing foreseeable risk of death or injury to 50+ people or over $1 billion in property damage). The bill drew near-universal opposition from labor unions, consumer advocates, and a formal House Democratic commission.
